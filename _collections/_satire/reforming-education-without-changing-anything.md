So you've been asked to reform education, and you have until Friday. Congratulations — this has been done many times before, and none of it requires touching a classroom.

**Step one: rename something.** "Grade 6" is tired. Call it "Foundational Stage III." Nobody needs to know what happens inside it differently, because nothing will, but the acronym alone will occupy three news cycles and one very confused principal who now has to reprint every signboard in the building.

**Step two: commission a new cover.** The old textbook was, by all accounts, mediocre. The solution is not to fix what's inside it — that would require curriculum specialists, field testing, and time you do not have before Friday. The solution is a new cover, ideally with a child looking optimistically at a globe. Reprint. Announce "revised and updated." Nobody will check page 47, where the content is identical to 2019, because nobody ever checks page 47.

**Step three: introduce an app.** Every reform needs a digital component, and the digital component need not function well, or at all, in schools without electricity, which is a separate problem you are not solving today. The app's job is not to teach anyone anything. Its job is to appear in a press photo, ideally held by a child who has never used it, smiling at a camera they were asked to smile at.

**Step four: hold a ceremony.** Invite people with sashes. A ceremony converts "we changed a font" into "we launched a new era in learning," and nobody at a ceremony asks pointed questions about teacher training budgets, because ceremonies are for clapping, not accounting.

**Step five: schedule the evaluation for after your term ends.** This is the most important step. Any honest measurement of whether Foundational Stage III actually taught anyone anything more than "Grade 6" did will take years — long enough that it becomes someone else's problem, announced with equal fanfare, wearing an equally new cover.

By Friday, you will have reformed education. Nothing will have changed inside a single classroom. But there will be a new sign on the gate, a new app icon on a tablet somewhere, and a photograph of a child holding a globe, and for most purposes, that is what reform has always meant.
