**SAJIDA:** You let them talk over each other for ten minutes and call it a lesson.

**IMRAN:** I call it an argument about a poem. That's more than they did in your class today.

**SAJIDA:** In my class today, every child could tell you what a metaphor is.

**IMRAN:** Could they tell you why anyone would bother using one?

**SAJIDA:** That comes later. First they need the tool.

**IMRAN:** You keep handing out tools nobody's asked for yet. I'd rather they wanted the hammer before I gave it to them.

**SAJIDA:** And if they never want it? Some of them won't, Imran. Some of them need it whether they want it or not, because the exam doesn't care what they wanted.

**IMRAN:** *(pause)* That's the part I don't have an answer for.

**SAJIDA:** I know.

**IMRAN:** It's not that your way is wrong. It's that I watched a boy today figure out, on his own, why the ending of that story felt unfair. Nobody handed him the word "irony." He got there without it, and then I gave him the word, and he lit up like I'd handed him a key to a door he'd already opened.

**SAJIDA:** That's a nice story, Imran.

**IMRAN:** It's not a story. It happened forty minutes ago.

**SAJIDA:** *(smiling despite herself)* Fine. Next term, you teach the first two weeks your way. I'll teach the two after that. We'll see whose class remembers more by December.

**IMRAN:** Whose class remembers more, or whose class can still stand each other's questions by December?

**SAJIDA:** Same thing, if you're doing it right.

**IMRAN:** *(after a beat)* Same thing if you're doing it right.

*They both go back to marking, in the same silence they started in, which is somehow not the same silence at all.*
