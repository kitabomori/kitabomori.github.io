<p>The Teaching Practice-III course of B.Ed-VI was the extension of Teaching Practice-I and II. In the last two practicums, we observed the different dimensions of the school, from gatekeeper to principal. We worked on developing lesson plans and delivered some lessons through co-teaching and micro-teaching.</p>

<p>The Teaching Practice-III course required the following objectives to be achieved:</p>

<ol><li>At least ten lessons are to be conducted at the allocated school.</li><li>The lesson plans need to be prepared following different models, e.g. KWL, 5E model, etc.</li><li>An observation sheet has to be filled out by the co-teacher to give feedback.</li><li>Reflections need to be written and submitted to the supervisor to highlight the learnings and loopholes of the lesson carried out.</li><li>A summative reflective report has to be written and submitted to the supervisor.</li><li>Finally, a hard portfolio that includes the lesson plans, observation sheets, reflections, and reflective report.</li></ol>

<p>The Teaching Practice-III course provided the opportunity to teach a full-fledged class at Government High School Number 01, Sukkur, for 10 weeks, starting on Feb 8, 2023. I taught mathematics and English to Section 7th-C in Urdu, and I also used English here and there, which some students found challenging to understand. In this reflective report, I discuss my experience of encountering several challenges and the lessons I learned from this teaching practice.</p>

<h4>Challenges Faced</h4>

<h4>1. Difficulty in Understanding Concepts</h4>

<p>I taught mathematics and English to grade 7. The mother tongue of the students is Sindhi, and I couldn't speak it. However, the textbooks are in English. Using Urdu and English to deliver was a tough task. The language barrier made it difficult for some students to understand the concepts being taught. To address this, I tried to explain concepts in the simplest terms, which helped some students understand better. I also provided additional time and explanations during break time to ensure that every student understood the concepts.</p>

<h4>2. Individual Attention</h4>

<p>One of the main challenges I faced during the lesson was providing attention to individual students. With a large class size, it was difficult to ensure that every student understood the basic concepts. Moreover, many of the students struggled to comprehend the lesson when the language shifted from Urdu to English, which made it even more challenging for me to communicate effectively. During break time, I tried to talk to the students who did not respond in class and asked for their feedback.</p>

<h4>3. Class Control and Engagement</h4>

<p>It was challenging to maintain class control and engage all students. Some students were not fully engaged and were disruptive at times, wanting to go to the washroom or to drink water, which took time to settle. This disrupted the lesson's flow and made it difficult to maintain the other students' attention. I tried to create an engaging lesson by incorporating hands-on learning and active learning techniques. Additionally, I tried to create a positive relationship with students to build a comfortable learning environment. However, some students still struggled to stay focused.</p>

<h4>Lessons Learned</h4>

<h4>1. Hands-on Learning and Time to Digest Information</h4>

<p>From this experience, I learned that hands-on learning/activities are necessary to teach students effectively. Active learning techniques, such as group discussions, problem-solving and role-playing, are effective in keeping students engaged and promoting a deeper understanding of the subject matter.</p>

<p>Furthermore, I realised that students need time to process information before moving on to the next topic. Therefore, it's essential to allocate sufficient time for students to reflect on what they have learned and ask questions. Moreover, there should not be a significant gap between one lesson and the next, to maintain continuity.</p>

<h4>2. Role of a Teacher</h4>

<p>I also realised that the role of a teacher is not just to teach, but to give students hope and a reason to learn. There is always a lack of curiosity to learn from the student side. Students need positive reinforcement and encouragement to develop curiosity and a desire to learn. It's important to build a positive relationship with students and foster a safe, supportive learning environment.</p>

<h4>3. Time Management and Lesson Planning</h4>

<p>During the lessons, I realised that students need time in class to process information before moving on to the next topic. At the same time, there should not be a large gap between one lesson and the next, but a teacher needs consistency in teaching methods and instructions to help students understand the concepts being taught.</p>

<p>I also learned that it's impossible to implement a lesson plan in its entirety. Unexpected situations, such as disruptive behaviour or unexpected student questions, can arise and alter the original plan. Therefore, it's essential to be flexible and adaptable in teaching methods and instruction to cater to each student's individual needs.</p>

<p>In conclusion, Teaching Practice III was a challenging experience. I tried to fulfil the course requirements by preparing lesson plans for each lesson and addressing the feedback from the observation sheets, plus writing a reflection on my teaching. Moreover, it provided me with some valuable insights into the teaching profession. I learned the importance of time management, lesson planning, active learning techniques and creating a positive relationship with students. Going forward, I am excited to continue learning and plan to use these insights to improve my teaching skills and become a more effective teacher.</p>
