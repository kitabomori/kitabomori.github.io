<p style="margin:1.5rem 0; padding:1rem; border-left:3px solid #009F93; font-size:0.95rem;">Practicum IV consisted of ten lessons at IBA Public School, Sukkur, teaching Grade VIII-Daisy (Science) and Grade VII-Rose (English).</p>

<h3>Invigilation Reflection</h3>

<p>The visit of 1st November to the IBA Public School Sukkur was a bit different from the past visits, where we had to deliver a lesson and then reflect on our teaching and how we manage the class. This time there were mid term exams and we were supposed to invigilate one of the classes. The coordinator on my wish sent me and my colleague to the girls 7th-Rose section, where the students had already started to attempt their mathematics paper. Following the due procedure of not disturbing the students I silently entered the examination hall which looked very intense with an invigilator and 48 students in it. I think the addition of two as for invigilation kind of made the examination hall environment more severe for the students.</p>

<p>The faces of the students were sad as it gave me the impression they were battling for their life or it was the mathematics paper that made it look so. I thought for a while what I could do to bring a smile to their faces and this thought took me to my matriculation and intermediate exams. At that time, the examiner tries to relax us by telling jokes or inspirational words at the start of the paper through the microphone. In between the break of paper-I and II at times we used to hear some poetry that clearly cheered up our faces. I wished I could have done that here but being for the first time on invigilation and unaware of the school examination rules refrain me from doing that. But somehow I end up writing "smile please" and drawing a happy face on the board. The second observation of mine is about the paper. The 100 marks paper was divided as paper-I and II, as objective based MCQs and subjective based CRQs and ERQs respectively. I doubt there is any chance of subjectivity in mathematics. Having a bit of knowledge about test development and evaluation made me analyze the paper. The paper-I was based on three chapters: set, algebra and coordinate geometry with 21, 16 and 13, MCQs respectively. This means there was unequal distribution of marks and importance was given to one or two chapters. The MCQs were mixed, and were not organized chapter wise making it more difficult to solve for the students. The paper-II was a bit better but there was no distinction between CRQs and ERQs as the questions were of the same type with the similar marks. There should be word problems in the ERQ section with higher marks as compared to CRQs. Finally, allocation of time for paper-I and II caught my attention. There were 50 minutes to attempt 50 MCQs of paper-I and an hour and ten minutes to attempt paper-II. At the end of the time for both the papers, students were pleading "sir one minute, sir one minute" as it usually happens in most of the examination halls. For a grade 7 student two hours are not enough to solve two papers of 100 marks. Hence, there is a need to pilot for time in order to set precise time for papers like mathematics which need time to calculate and check answers twice. When I talked to the respective teacher who set the paper for the class, she agreed with some of the highlighted points and gave a thumbs up to give a thought on this.</p>

<p>For me the examination hall has always been a time to observe and enjoy the looks, peers make while attempting a paper. As a prospective teacher this experience and the serious faces of students made me realize we (students) are fearful of exams and it is a question mark on our education system. In an examination hall it is vital to help students to feel at ease so that they would attempt the exam by taking it as a daily routine practice.</p>
