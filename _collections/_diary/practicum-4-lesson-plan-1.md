<h4>2. Required Material</h4>
<ul>
  <li>Periodic table</li>
  <li>Whiteboard and markers</li>
  <li>Handouts with periodic table information/book</li>
</ul>

<h4>3. Getting Started</h4>
<ul>
  <li>Asked why we are arranging things in an organised way. For example, we arrange books on shelves and clothes in wardrobes. Plate, kitchen utensils, and cupboards. On what basis do we arrange/put things in one place?</li>
  <li>Could you tell me the names of some common elements?</li>
</ul>

<h4>4. Activity 1: Deliberation</h4>
<ul>
  <li>Display the periodic table on the whiteboard and ask the student to observe and ask why the elements are arranged in that particular manner.</li>
</ul>

<h4>5. Activity 2: Lecture</h4>
<p><strong>Groups</strong> are vertical columns in the periodic table. Emphasise that elements in the same group share similar properties. Give examples of elements in the same group, such as the noble gases (Group 18), alkali metals (Group 1), and alkaline earth metals (Group 2).</p>
<p><strong>Periods</strong> are horizontal rows in the periodic table. Elements in the same period don't necessarily have similar properties, but their properties change in a pattern as you move across a period. You can illustrate this by comparing elements in the same period, such as hydrogen and helium in the first period, lithium, beryllium, boron, and so on.</p>
<p><strong>Trends Across Periods</strong>: Discuss some of the trends that occur as you move across a period, such as increasing atomic number, increasing atomic mass, and changes in properties like atomic size and reactivity.</p>

<h4>6. Conclusion</h4>
<ul>
  <li>Summarise the main points about the periodic trends in Groups I, II, and VII.</li>
  <li>Ask students to reflect on how atomic structure influences these trends.</li>
  <li>Assign homework or further reading to reinforce the concepts learned during the lesson.</li>
  <li>Encourage questions and clarify any doubts students may have.</li>
</ul>
