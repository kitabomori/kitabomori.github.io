<div style="text-align:right; font-size:0.85rem; color:#666; margin:0 0 1rem 0;">November 15, 2023</div>

<p>It takes time for a teacher to adjust to new situations, both to teach and to learn from students. It became even more difficult when a teacher did not know the students' learning styles. Today, at X School, Sukkur, I was in the 7th-X section, packed with 42 female students. Not having experience teaching girls in previous practicums was a challenge for me. As usual, I stopped by the class during break time to interact with the students, but most were outside having lunch. I chatted with a few students and got to know them. The English lesson was based on a VARK reading activity that had students explore their preferred learning styles.</p>

<p>While involving the students in the task of reading and completing the VARK worksheet, I was constantly thinking about how easy it would be for a teacher to teach a group of students with the same learning style. For example, a group of students love to read and discuss the characters of stories. It would be very effective, especially when learning a second language, as students would put the language into practice. In such a situation, teachers would not shout at students to pay attention to the board or to keep quiet. But unfortunately, as teachers, we do not consider this aspect: that students should be allowed and supported in learning the way they prefer. In Neil Fleming's 1987 VARK model, there are four learning styles/modes—first, the Visual learners, who like diagrams, pictures, slides, colours, and symbols. Second, the Aural/Auditory prefers stories and ideas to be discussed and explained out loud. Third, the Read/Write styles, where learners rely on handouts, notes, manuals, and workbooks. And finally, the Kinesthetic learning style: students love field trips, labs, real-life examples, the trial-and-error method, and problem-solving. It is not set in stone that students will have just one style. They could learn more than one style. During the class, I used the whiteboard to connect with visual learners, spoke and answered questions in English and Urdu simultaneously to focus on aural learners, and provided the reading and the worksheet to incorporate read/write learning styles into the learning process. But I could not think of any way to help kinesthetic learners; that means I am not able to facilitate their learning of something new.</p>

<p>It is of great importance to me as a teacher to understand students' learning styles. Which style do they prefer to learn, or that excites them, so they will be fully engaged in learning when the teacher facilitates? Being aware of students' learning styles would enable a teacher to plan and incorporate activities into the class accordingly.</p>
