<h4>1. General Objectives</h4>
<p>Students will be able to:</p>
<ul>
  <li>develop mathematical curiosity when solving problems.</li>
  <li>become confident in using mathematics to analyse and solve problems both in school and in real-life situations.</li>
  <li>develop the knowledge, skills, and attitudes necessary to pursue further studies in mathematics.</li>
</ul>

<h4>2. Specific Objective (SLOs)</h4>
<p>Students will be able to:</p>
<ul>
  <li>recall what a fraction is.</li>
  <li>differentiate between types as a proper, improper, and mixed fraction.</li>
  <li>develop the concept of equivalent fractions.</li>
  <li>solve the addition or subtraction of fractions with the same and different denominators.</li>
</ul>

<h4>3. Materials & Resources</h4>
<p>Blackboard, different colour chalks, textbook, different charts/sticky notes to represent fractions 4-5 apples, and a knife.</p>

<h4>4. Preparedness (4 minutes)</h4>
<p>The teacher will greet the students and before going to the topic, he will inquire about their daily chores and activities. Furthermore, the teacher before the announcement of the topic will ask some questions related to fractions, which will be:</p>
<ol>
  <li>How are you doing these days?</li>
  <li>Are you enthusiastic about the summers or sad?</li>
  <li>If I gave you an apple and asked you to cut it into four equal pieces and you eat one piece how much would be left?</li>
</ol>

<h4>5. Introduction (5 Minutes)</h4>
<p>As we ate an apple now let's see the topic which we are going to study today "fraction".</p>
<ol>
  <li>Do you remember the fraction that you have previously studied?</li>
  <li>Can any of you tell us all what is a fraction?</li>
</ol>

<h4>6. Lecture (11 minutes)</h4>
<p>After knowing the prior knowledge of the students, the teacher will come to the main topic i.e addition and subtraction of fractions. The teacher will first explain the concept of numerator and denominator, then the teacher will solve examples on page number 33 of the textbook. After that, the teacher will solve and explain questions where the denominator will be the same and then another one with a different denominator.</p>

<h4>7. Discussion and Questions (10 minutes)</h4>
<p>As the teacher explains the concept of addition and subtraction now the students will be asked about their confusion. This time practice method will be used to explain the concepts. The co-teacher will assist the teacher and will provide help to students even when needed individually to solve questions of exercise 3.1 on page number 37 of the textbook.</p>

<h4>8. Assessment</h4>
<p>The teacher will provide sticky notes to the students to either represent, add or subtract a fraction during the class. The teacher with the assistance of a co-teacher will observe the students solving questions.</p>

<h4>9. Homework</h4>
<p>The students will be assigned to solve part and b of exercise 3.1. And they will be instructed to mark the questions which they wouldn't be able to solve.</p>

<p style="font-style:italic; color:#666;">Note: The lesson was delivered by the teacher.</p>

<hr style="margin:2rem 0; border:none; border-top:1px solid #ddd;">

<h4>Feedback by Co-teacher H</h4>

<p>The student-teacher had a lesson plan. He delivered all the information which he had included in his lesson plan. However, certain areas need improvement. Some of them are as follows:</p>

<ul><li><strong>Student Engagement:</strong> In primary classes, as it is important to create a sense of belonging in the classroom, a very simple strategy is naming the children while giving examples. As the topic was fractions, an example could be: Moiz has 1 banana and divides it among his friends, Amal, Ana, and Elif. This will involve the students in the example and create a sense of belonging in them.</li><li><strong>Consistent pace:</strong> The student-teacher gave a lot of examples to make the students understand the concept of fractions. However, the students of grade 5 seem already familiar with the concept, so fewer examples are actually required.</li><li>For those students who couldn't grasp the information, many times the strategy of moving ahead works. It means that if they haven't understood the concept at the moment, they might understand a little later as the questions are solved. Too many examples can make things complex even for those who have already grasped the concept. If they still don't understand after solving the questions, a specific time shall be given to them to help them understand.</li></ul>

<p>Based on my analysis, these areas can be improved or were slightly weak and can be strengthened.</p>

<h4>Feedback by Co-teacher N</h4>

<p>The Teacher, while giving the lecture, needs to explain every aspect of the topic and avoid mixing up the concepts of like and unlike fractions. At first, he tried to explain the basic concepts of the numerator and denominator because most students did not understand the concept the teacher was trying to convey. And try to give more examples to convey the topic's concept clearly.</p>

<p>The teacher's body language and energy delivery were good, and he tried to explain every detail. If some students did not understand, he would try to explain again and again, which was a good thing.</p>
