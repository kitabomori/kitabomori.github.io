<p>Have you ever thought about what time is? For some, it's an illusion; for some, an invention to sell clocks; but for some, it's an elephant, and we're blind, with very limited knowledge, throwing our observations at only the part of the elephant we have access to — only the part we can take hold of. I asked, "What is time for you?" and had students in B.Ed-VIII write their responses on Mentimeter to start the lesson on "time dilation" in physics. I put forward the twin paradox and the phenomenon of time dilation that explains it. With this, the second student teacher explained the frame of reference for observing objects at rest and in motion. He threw a ball to help students understand how speed, time, and distance are relative, and how the Lorentz factor proves it. He then shared the possibility of time travel for passengers on a fast-moving spacecraft as a dramatic application of time dilation, and closed the lesson there.</p>

<p>The purpose of teaching time dilation was to familiarise student-teachers with one of the most complicated yet intriguing phenomena of relative motion, and to make students realise there is so much still to learn. If we want to really teach students physics, we must have knowledge that makes them wonder and opens up the possibility of imagining the unthinkable. The supervisor remarked, "Very good content knowledge, and you presented the topic confidently," with nothing noted in the checklist's suggestion section — certainly a boost to our confidence. Strong content knowledge is generally considered effective for instruction, but paired with students' genuine willingness to learn, it makes a lesson far more productive. We concluded this because we managed to engage students for most of the lesson, though their interest faded toward the end, perhaps because some were preparing to teach their own lesson right after ours.</p>

<p>This was our last lesson, completing the ten-lesson practicum requirement. We're not sure whether there will be another, but this was the last one for us as a pair. We don't have to share what we'd teach next, or how we'd improve the next class. The only thing left to say is that we enjoyed learning to teach, and teaching to learn. Whatever we've learned, we'll carry forward as we keep teaching and learning in the days ahead.</p>
