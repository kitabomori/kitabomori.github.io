<p>Over the 16 weeks of teaching practice, we were supposed to visit the school 12 to 14 times, or conduct micro-teaching at Sukkur IBA University when we didn't go to the site. In the end, only 10 lesson plans, along with observation checklists and reflections on each lesson, needed to be submitted as an e-portfolio to the course supervisor to meet the course requirements. Throughout, the blend of classroom observations and post-lesson conferences was pivotal in making our teaching effective, understanding students' behaviour, and shaping our instructional strategies to engage them.</p>

<p>We invested our energy in simplifying physics concepts through simple demonstrations. Selecting texts — excerpts from diaries, letters, and books — for lessons in Urdu and English also aimed to give students a sense of what kind of literature they should be reading. Telling students about the use of flowers during times of war sparked their interest while teaching history. To make mathematics, often considered the most boring subject, more engaging, we discussed its applications in daily life. Taking an interest in students' curiosities after class also showed that when a teacher appreciates them and provides relevant answers, students respect their teachers in turn.</p>

<p>Throughout the bachelor's degree programme, there were several practicum courses, and this one marked the end of school visits to learn to teach and put into practice what we had learned. Each practicum taught us many things and improved our teaching, but this one was peculiar for several reasons that also made it enjoyable. First, we went to class in pairs, with one of us teaching and the other observing — a different experience from previous practicums, where cooperating teachers observed with little interest in or understanding of how prospective teachers should be observed. We observed each other and offered suggestions to improve our mishaps, considering both the circumstances and the theories we'd learned. The post-conference was also a novel experience, where we discussed the strengths and weaknesses of the lesson just taught, and shared difficulties with the course supervisor to explore possible solutions.</p>

<p>Despite that, observation has its limitations. We were required to observe based on a provided checklist that doesn't account for a class's circumstances or a student teacher's philosophy. For instance, the checklist demands a variety of strategies within a lesson, but doing so in a class of 40 students creates nothing but havoc — we witnessed it firsthand. We held to a philosophy of engaging students in meaningful learning, without fixating on completing every planned activity on time, but the checklist would regard such lessons as ineffective and incomplete. Lesson plans should explicitly state a teacher's teaching philosophy, and be judged with classroom circumstances in mind.</p>

<p>We also noticed, in observing classes and cooperating teachers, that the cooperating school ran on its own set of beliefs and rules — we're not sure if they're part of any school policy document, but we witnessed a couple of disruptive incidents that limited a teacher's authority. When someone interferes with a class's proceedings, whether the principal or a teacher, it's unacceptable to us. It disrupts the class, students lose attention, and it becomes very difficult to bring them back to the topic. It also amounts to treating a teacher as though they have no worth or value in the very room that is meant to be their playfield.</p>

<p>Observations and post-conferences helped us identify the strengths and weaknesses of each lesson and prepare more effectively for the next. In one physics lesson, we used worksheets to assess whether students had learned a particular concept; distributing them in groups threw the whole lesson off. In the post-conference, we reflected that group work would be much harder in such a crowded class, so activities needed to be individual instead. That discussion guided the rest of our lessons and spared us further disruption. Some students also showed disruptive behaviour, and we had trouble completing lesson plans within 35 minutes, holding students' attention, and catering to individual needs. At times it felt like all the pedagogy, assessment methods, and classroom management techniques we'd learned were useless. To work through this, we approached our supervisor for feedback, reflected on our methods and plans, and developed lessons with students' interests in mind. Through constant feedback and support for one another, we refined our approach and adapted to students' needs. Changing sections from VIII-Daisy to Tulip also helped tremendously — the latter students may have received the Sindh Educational Foundation scholarship after a rigorous selection process, but they were also curious and serious about learning.</p>

<p>In the end, observing lessons and taking part in post-conferences gave us practical field insight into teaching, learning, and what actually engages students. It made us realise how different the roles of a teacher and a prospective teacher really are — and how each role demands different strategies to face its own challenges. A prospective teacher can step out of a classroom when circumstances turn unfavourable; a teacher cannot. A teacher has to stay and manage the disruption. Whether it's right to change a class into a practice-teaching session at all remains, to us, an open question.</p>

<p>We'd recommend planning the practicum to genuinely prepare prospective teachers for the role ahead, rather than treating it as a checklist of lessons to deliver, observations to sit through, reflections to write, and a report to summarise it all. Elementary teachers have to teach several subjects, so within a single teaching practice course, only one or two subjects should be the core focus, so prospective teachers can properly master them; planned well across all five teaching practice courses, there's ample time to eventually cover every subject. We'd also design the school visits in two phases: consecutive visits for a full week in the first half of the semester, then again in the second half — six lessons with observations and post-conference reflections happening back to back would do far more to connect one lesson to the next. In the gap between, prospective teachers would also get time to reflect and explore other options, and get more used to the actual rhythm and routine of a cooperating school. We believe the life of a prospective teacher, while practising, should resemble the real working life of a teacher and a school far more closely than it currently does.</p>
