<p style="margin:1.5rem 0; padding:1rem; border-left:3px solid #009F93; font-size:0.95rem;">Practicum-II grouped three student teachers together to plan and teach a lesson: one teaches while the other two observe and give feedback. In these posts, the author is referred to as “the teacher,” and the other two as “co-teacher H” and “co-teacher N.” The identities of the actual teacher and the two schools involved have been omitted.</p>

<p>Practicum-II is a step up from Practicum-I, which involved several visits to the X and Y Government School in Old Sukkur. During those visits, we observed and understood the roles of various aspects of the school, including the building, labs and laboratories, library, classrooms, management, teachers, the environment, and all staff who support the learning and teaching process. It also emphasises co-teaching and receiving feedback from co-teachers to further strengthen it.</p>

<p>The supervisor taught us how to plan and implement a lesson in the first two sessions. To prepare lesson plans, learn about the students, and identify facilities that could be used during co-teaching, we visited two schools selected by the Department of Education. Both are primary schools, but one is in Urdu and the other in Sindhi. The supervisor divided the student-teachers into groups based on whether they wanted to teach in an Urdu- or Sindhi-medium school. I end up at an Urdu-medium school with some other students. We went to the assigned grade 5th class, per the supervisor's instructions and with the permission of the school principal. For a while, I observed the teacher's teaching style and gained insight into her methods and students. During the break, I talked with the teacher about the students and the school. Finally, as a group, we decided to teach mathematics in the coming weeks and asked the teacher for a copy of the textbook.</p>

<p>Visiting the respective school gave me a perspective that appears very challenging. First and foremost, the school lacks necessities such as clean water and washrooms. Second, there is a scarcity of instructional aids used in the teaching and learning process. This is a huge challenge; now I must focus on no- or low-cost instructional materials to deliver a meaningful lesson. Yes, the circumstances are difficult, but that is the reality of education in our country. That's why one of my instructors used to say, "Teaching is not everyone's cup of tea." I hope to deliver an effective lesson by the end of the course.</p>
