The teaching literacy course has given me a whole new perspective on literacy, enabling me to interpret it in new ways. Literacy is an umbrella term that encompasses knowledge of practically everything, from oneself to others, from one's family to strangers and from one's well-being to inspiring individuals to revolt against oppressive powers. Hence, the acknowledged definition of literacy, the ability to read and write, has become more important for me than before. Reading provides awareness, food for thought, and highlights society's wrongdoings. While writing is the most effective way to organise ideas, communicate with clarity, and express your perspective with confidence for greater impact.

Every single activity begins somewhere; unfortunately, my memory does not help me recall how I started reading and writing. Formal schooling has taught me to memorise some letters in Urdu and English, which I suppose is not writing or reading, but a prerequisite. Until grade eight, I may have had positions in class, but not achieved literacy. Then there was a new school, a novel environment, and, particularly, a unique English teacher, who made me realise why reading and writing are important. Often, the teacher would ask, "What are you reading nowadays?" If you come up with a book name, he would ask about a particular character and certain settings. It was impossible to tell a lie, as the teacher had been a student at the same school and had probably gone through all the novels and the library's literature section. To be on the safe side, I started my first-ever book, “The Young Adventures and the Hollow Tree” by Enid Blyton. The children's book was in hardcover, and it took me more than a month to finish the two hundred odd pages.

The following year, another English teacher motivated and fostered a love of reading by saying that readers are leaders and by discussing leaders who were avid readers. Most of my classmates regularly borrow and lend books. A few of the mates used to read 40 to 50 pages an hour. I wish I had been able to read consistently to develop my language skills. Still, something is better than nothing. At the end of my college year, I had developed a sense of reading for pleasure, and that is still with me.

As I started university education, reading came to a halt, and I focused on writing. The English content courses, one and two, mainly focus on writing flawlessly by emphasising grammar and punctuation, but I got nowhere. The English content three-course again focused on reading, and soon I realised that without reading, writing cannot be improved. That is where I make some conscious efforts to read for writing and to write. Near the end of the course, I wrote a satire titled “The Right Way to Shit on…” which questioned the education system that individuals reach to universities, but still don’t develop the sense of how to use toilets. I presented the satire in class, and I am still proud of it today.

It has taken a lot of time and effort. All the reading, writing, and other forms of literacy have helped shape the person I am today. Literacy has helped me to become more aware of my surroundings. Recently, I proposed suggestions to improve the administrative and day-to-day operations of a department of my university. What I observed was revealing: those in positions of authority often feel threatened by people who raise legitimate concerns and point out specific problems. Rather than engaging with the substance of the issues, they respond by asking, "Who are you to question or tell us what to do?" and, at times, attempt to silence criticism through intimidation or by invoking authority.

To me, this raises an important question about leadership, accountability, and literacy. If a person is educated and committed to the public good, should they remain silent in the face of clear problems, or do they have a responsibility to speak up? I continue to seek an answer to this dilemma: why is constructive criticism so often perceived as a threat rather than an opportunity for improvement?
