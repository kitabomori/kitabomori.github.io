<h3>Lesson IX Reflection</h3>

<p>November 29 was the day we visited the IBA Public School, Sukkur, for the ninth time, which was supposed to be our second-last excursion of teaching practice. I developed a lesson to help students in 7th-Rose appreciate the concept of free writing after analysing my students' VARK data. I started the lesson by instructing the students on a prompt story: I began with the exposition, then the students added whatever came to mind. Moving towards free writing, I wrote three words, "adventure, mystery and laughter," on the board. Still, I instructed them to put pen to paper for 10 minutes, forgetting about punctuation, spelling, sentence structure, and the use of heavy vocabulary in their writing.</p>

<p>This lesson offered me some exciting insights as a prospective teacher. First of all, students love writing when there is no fear of being judged, graded or punished for writing a wrong sentence. The students were as busy as bees when they were writing, and no one was making any disturbance. A few students wrote a full page of a story, and others tried to write something of their own for the first time. Writing has always fascinated me as a student and teacher. I don't write much, which isn't a good thing, but when my students write, I feel complete. Secondly, I found that students value themselves when they are appreciated beyond academics and respect the teacher who helped them bring out the best in themselves. Today, as a male, I spent time in the break with the girls. That is, in itself, a taboo in this context, and to appreciate them is beyond imagination. When I brought their outside lives into class, they felt valued, and that is what teaching is all about. The way they played basketball and enjoyed the sport was something to be happy about.</p>

<p>Interacting with students made me realise what Bell Hooks had said years back. An academy (school/classroom) is not a paradise where students want to be, but a teacher can create one where they can enjoy learning, which is missing in our schools. I wonder what a teacher could do to make students happy? The start could be to spend more and more time in breaks, and whenever a teacher finds an opportunity, instead of gossiping in the corners of staff rooms.</p>
