<p><strong>Students will be able to:</strong></p><ul><li><strong>I.</strong> recall what an angle is <em>(30%)</em></li><li><strong>II.</strong> recognise types of angles such as acute, right, obtuse, and straight angles <em>(35%)</em></li><li><strong>III.</strong> solve problems related to the angles <em>(35%)</em></li></ul>

<p style="font-size:0.8rem; color:#888;">(R = remembering, U = understanding, A = applying, An = analysing, E = evaluating, C = creating)</p>

<h4>2. Required Material</h4>

<p>Board (white/black), chalk/marker, duster.</p>

<h4>5E Model</h4>

<p><strong>Engagement:</strong> Start the lesson by asking the students if they have ever heard of an acute, obtuse, right, straight, complementary, or supplementary angle. Write the word “Angle” on the board and ask the students to brainstorm different types of angles they know.</p>

<p><strong>Exploration:</strong> Distribute angle-measuring tools (protractors) to each student. Have the students explore how to use the tool by measuring the angles on their worksheets or flashcards. Assist the students in measuring angles, reminding them how to align the protractor properly and where to read the measurements.</p>

<p><strong>Explanation:</strong> Review the measurements of each angle with the students and then introduce them to the different types of angles — acute, right, obtuse, and straight — explaining the characteristics and definitions of each.</p>

<p><strong>Elaboration:</strong> Provide students with real-world scenarios where they can identify different types of angles, such as the angle formed between the hands on a clock or the angle formed by a street intersection. Have students work in pairs or small groups to create their own scenarios and challenge each other to identify the types of angles within them.</p>

<p><strong>Evaluation:</strong> Distribute a short quiz or worksheet to assess the students' understanding of the different types of angles.</p>
