Imagine a rose plant cutting, buried two-thirds of it in a bed to grow a new one. In the bed, various flowers are already growing, probably not of the same colour and shape. The bed has all the necessary minerals, along with exposure to sunlight and a constant supply of water. There is also a gardener who, once in a while, only sees the cutting. With time, would the cutting grow into a plant, forming roots and shoots? It's complicated to answer. Weeds would grow, preventing sunlight from reaching the cutting, and it would ultimately die out, losing its capacity to grow into a plant. In a second case, when a good gardener starts to nurture the cutting and the already-grown flower, facilitating the natural process by removing weeds and protecting it from being eaten by sheep or other animals. The rose cutting, one day, would fully grow into a plant and add its own beauty and fragrance, along with other flowers, to the garden.

If the rose cutting is replaced by a child (student) and taught by a school, then there is always a need for a good teacher to facilitate the growth and learning. And this is what I believe and strive to be as a teacher: to get rid of the weeds and let the sunlight reach the plant so it can realise its innate potential. I believe that each child has the potential to learn, grow, and serve when provided with suitable environments and supported by a teacher. It is not always possible to appreciate, listen to, and care for the needs of each and every student in the classroom within 40 minutes. Therefore, it is vital to be available to the students. It's healthy to remain among students and interact with them at playgrounds and other places, becoming their friend and classmate. This results in the removal of any unwanted barriers students might have to inquire and learn, and ends stereotypical thoughts.

I am never in favour of being an authoritarian manager to maintain classroom decorum and deposit information in students, which Paulo Freire calls the banking model of education. Not allowing students to express themselves or pursue their interests, sticking to a single teaching method, and failing to engage students based on individual differences result in learning nothing new. Such practices create a living hell, and that is why Bell Hooks might have said this. "The academy is not paradise, but learning is a place where paradise can be created". Teaching should be engaging and relevant to students' interests. It is also the responsibility of teachers to develop awareness of what is happening in their surroundings, which should lead them to reflect and take action (Freireian praxis).

A teacher must do a job to foster creativity. To achieve this aim, it's effective to embrace students' mistakes and create a learning environment that encourages them to take risks. Promoting collaborative learning helps students express, challenge, and refine their thought processes. By listening to peers, students also encounter new perspectives that broaden their horizons and deepen their understanding.

I have the feeling that education should enable an individual to qualify, socialise, and develop subjectivity. According to Biesta (2010), qualification concerns the transmission and acquisition of knowledge, skills, and dispositions. It enables individuals to earn by doing something. Socialisation is about the thorough transmission of community knowledge, norms and values through interaction, which is thought to be part of the hidden curriculum. While subjectification refers to the process through which individuals become subjective beings, developing a sense of identity, agency and subjectivity.

Conclusively, my teaching philosophy includes the engaged pedagogy of Bell Hooks and the emancipatory process of Paulo Freire, as I believe that contextual and engaging practices that transgress the language and authoritative ideologies in the classroom utilise the students' lived realities to better facilitate learning. Moreover, the work of Gert Biesta plays a vital role in my teaching philosophy, as I wholeheartedly stand for the development of students' skills, agency, and self-expression, so that their identity is formed, while also emphasising the role of socialisation and the development of strong social factors.
