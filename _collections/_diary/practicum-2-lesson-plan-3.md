<h4>1. General Objectives</h4>
<p>Students will be able to:</p>
<ul>
  <li>develop mathematical curiosity when solving problems.</li>
  <li>become confident in using mathematics to analyse and solve problems both in school and in real-life situations.</li>
  <li>develop the knowledge, skills, and attitudes necessary to pursue further studies in mathematics.</li>
</ul>

<h4>2. Specific Objectives (Students Learning Outcomes)</h4>
<p>Students will be able to:</p>
<ul>
  <li>recall solving fractions with the same denominator.</li>
  <li>differentiate between fractions with the same and different denominators.</li>
  <li>solve the addition or subtraction of fractions with different denominators.</li>
</ul>

<h4>3. Materials & Resources</h4>
<p>Blackboard, different colour chalks, and textbook.</p>

<h4>4. Greetings (4 Minutes)</h4>
<p>The teacher will greet the students and before going to the topic the teacher will inquire about their prior knowledge regarding solving fractions with the same denominator. The teacher will ask the students if they have any difficulty or confusion with the previous topic. After a short review of the previous lesson, the teacher will move on to the topic.</p>

<h4>5. Introduction (2 Minutes)</h4>
<p>As the students are already familiar with the concept of fractions with different denominators so the teacher will move ahead with the topic.</p>

<h4>6. Lesson (12 Minutes)</h4>
<p>The teacher will deliver the lesson with the help of examples given in the book on page number 33 example no 1.</p>
<p>The teacher will explain the method of solving fractions with different denominators, such as taking LCM and then simply adding or subtracting them. There are numerous examples available in the book after their solution on the board the teacher will move to the exercise. As the exercise is quite lengthy, the questions which are more confusing will be solved on the board. In particular, questions involving three terms as given in the exercise part b and part d.</p>

<h4>7. Discussion and Questions (14 Minutes)</h4>
<p>While solving questions on the board the teacher will ask the students verbally to tell her the procedure. Furthermore, the teacher will ask students who are willing to solve questions on the board. This will help the teacher to identify where students get confused or cannot solve questions.</p>

<h4>8. Assessment</h4>
<p>The teacher will assess the students, and they will reflect upon their teaching method. The teacher with the assistance of a co-teacher will observe the students solving questions that will be an assessment.</p>

<h4>9. Homework (3 minutes)</h4>
<p>The teacher will specify some questions from the exercise for homework.</p>

<p style="font-style:italic; color:#666;">Note: Co-teacher H delivered the lesson.</p>

<h4>Feedback to Co-teacher H</h4>

<p>On what basis should I give you feedback, given that the intended lesson plan was not implemented in the respective class? The following is general feedback that highlights the sparkling areas and a few of the hollow ones.</p>

<p>There were many positives to take away from today's class:</p>

<ul><li>The ability to make quick decisions. You did not even take a minute to decide what to do (tables and then addition) or how to teach grade two when the intended class, 5th, was wholly absent. Such ability is vital in teaching, as things never go as planned.</li><li>There was scorching temperature, and you were full of energy. You were continuously interacting with students for one hour and forty-five minutes, excluding the poems and playtime, which certainly needs appreciation.</li><li>Your voice was audible to the whole class.</li><li>The other ability was to mingle with students. I guess they didn't feel any difficulty, since you were delivering a lecture for the first time.</li><li>Bringing students to the board and asking them to solve questions, then using rewards to praise their performance, was a good strategy. I believe it has given tons of confidence to lots of students who were afraid to come forward.</li></ul>

<p>As the lesson was a spontaneous one, there's nothing to critique, but a few points that might help you strengthen your teaching further:</p>

<ul><li>There was limited movement on your part during the whole class — concentrated around the blackboard area. It's worth remembering to move around the whole class to have a glance.</li><li>Initially, you started to teach about tables, then switched to addition. At this age, it might not be easy for grade two students to comprehend two topics and make connections between them.</li><li>One more thing that could have been done was using the concepts of units, tens, and hundredths to further clarify the concept of carrying tens during addition.</li><li>A short break after one hour or 45 minutes could have been appreciated, giving some time for the tiny brain to digest the information.</li></ul>

<p>I hope this feedback will help bring a little improvement.</p>
