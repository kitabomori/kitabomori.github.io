<h4>1. General Objectives</h4>

<p>Students will be able to:</p>

<ul><li>develop mathematical curiosity when solving problems.</li><li>become confident in using mathematics to analyse and solve problems both in school and in real-life situations.</li><li>develop the knowledge, skills, and attitudes necessary to pursue further studies in mathematics.</li></ul>

<h4>2. Specific Objectives (SLOs)</h4>

<p>Students will be able to:</p>

<ul><li>recall solving fractions with the same denominator.</li><li>differentiate between fractions with the same and different denominators.</li><li>solve the addition or subtraction of fractions with different denominators.</li></ul>

<h4>3. Materials &amp; Resources</h4>

<p>Blackboard, different coloured chalks, and a textbook.</p>

<h4>4. Greetings (4 minutes)</h4>

<p>The teacher will greet the students and, before going to the topic, will inquire about their prior knowledge of solving fractions with the same denominator. The teacher will ask the students if they have any difficulty or confusion with the previous topic. After a short review of the previous lesson, the teacher will move on to the topic.</p>

<h4>5. Introduction (2 minutes)</h4>

<p>As the students are already familiar with fractions with different denominators, the teacher will move on to the topic.</p>

<h4>6. Lesson (12 minutes)</h4>

<p>The teacher will deliver the lesson using examples from the book on page 33, example no. 1. The teacher will explain the method for solving fractions with different denominators, such as finding the LCM and then adding or subtracting them. There are numerous examples in the book. After their solution on the board, the teacher will move to the exercise. As the exercise is quite lengthy, the more confusing questions will be solved on the board — in particular, questions involving three terms from the exercise, parts b and d.</p>

<h4>7. Discussion and Questions (14 minutes)</h4>

<p>While solving questions on the board, the teacher will ask the students verbally to tell her the procedure. Furthermore, the teacher will ask students who are willing to solve questions on the board. This will help the teacher identify where students get confused or cannot solve questions.</p>

<h4>8. Assessment</h4>

<p>The teacher will assess the students, and they will reflect upon their teaching method. The teacher, with the assistance of a co-teacher, will observe students solving assessment questions.</p>

<h4>9. Homework (3 minutes)</h4>

<p>The teacher will specify some questions from the exercise for homework.</p>

<ul><li>The ability to make quick decisions. You did not even take a minute to decide what to do (tables and then addition) or how to teach grade two when the intended class, 5th, was wholly absent. <p>As the lesson was a spontaneous one, there's nothing to critique, but a few points that might help you strengthen your teaching further:</p>

It's worth remembering to move around the whole class to have a glance.</li><li>Initially, you started to teach about tables, then switched to addition. At this age, it might not be easy for grade two students to comprehend two topics and make connections between them.</li><li>One more thing that could have been done was using the concepts of units, tens, and hundredths to further clarify the concept of carrying tens during addition.</li><li>A short break after one hour or 45 minutes could have been appreciated, giving some time for the tiny brain to digest the information.</li></ul>

<p>I hope this feedback will help bring a little improvement.</p>
