<div style="text-align:right; font-size:0.85rem; color:#666; margin:0 0 1rem 0;">(October 11, 2023)</div>

<p>I think things never go as we plan or want them to. It doesn't mean I didn't curate my lesson plan or enjoy teaching at X School, Sukkur, with the students of grade 8th. There is a break and a physical training (PT) period before science class. Today, during the break, I was at the football ground with the students for a while and observed their behaviour. While in the PT period, I was in the cemented area right behind the boys' section building and played cricket with the students for the entire period. Unfortunately, my team lost the five-over cricket match. Still, the particular experience of being with students both outside (playing with the students) and inside (carrying out the lesson plan) class forced me to question what kind of prospective teacher I am and how my teaching practice is shaping my identity.</p>

<p>In the first place, I felt I was in pieces and questioned myself. Should I be a teacher who teaches what is in the test book, or a teacher who has the agency to go beyond the boundaries for students' learning? And certainly I have to answer this by myself. I witnessed joy and eagerness to perform better, and the students were giving their hundred per cent while playing, but once inside the class, they were not attentive. Is it because our prospective teachers' lesson plans focus only on the cognitive domain of knowledge acquisition? There is hardly any conscious effort in the affective and psychomotor domains, which makes learning boring. To overcome this boredom, students indulge in destructive activities, which ultimately leads to an unwillingness to learn. Secondly, there is a dire need to spend time with students. While playing and having small kachari (conversation) with the students, I came to know about their peer groups and their feelings. One particular student told me he has only two friends among 33 students, and the others never appreciate him playing or include him on their teams. This is a significant loss for the student's personal development, as he feels segregated. According to Erikson, a lack of encouragement can foster a sense of inferiority in children, ultimately leading them to doubt their abilities. Lastly, students need a bit of motivation to learn. Students can be motivated by small talk or a shabash (praise) for a small act within the class or during activities they do outside. But unfortunately, we don't give a damn about anything outside of achieving particular learning outcomes.</p>

<p>As bell hooks rightly said, "the academy is not paradise, but learning is a place where paradise can be created". Moving ahead, it is of utmost importance for me to develop a lesson that focuses on all domains of Bloom's taxonomy to engage students mentally, physically, and emotionally in learning. Learning can only be joyful when the teacher praises and encourages the small acts in class and in other activities. Otherwise, students would be present in the classroom, but the outer world would capture their imaginations, and they would consume their energy learning nothing.</p>
