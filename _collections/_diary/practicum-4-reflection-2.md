<div style="text-align:right; font-size:0.85rem; color:#666; margin:0 0 1rem 0;">(September 27, 2023)</div>

<p>I had planned to teach about sound loudness and how to measure it using a mobile sound meter app. The observation sheet and the supervising teacher's comments are encouraging, as they indicate some improvement over the previous lesson. I maintained the board and wrote the lesson topic, but I still forgot to write the subtopic. I planned to assess the learning using a worksheet, but due to time constraints/mismanagement, I went to the students individually and in pairs to ask questions orally. To sum it all up, the class went smoothly, but there are a few observations/shortcomings that are important to address in the coming lessons.</p>

<p>Foremost, I doubt the teaching method in this case, Inductive, was right for today's class. I started the lesson by generally asking how sound is produced and how it propagates from one place to another. In other words, I approached the topic from a general perspective, and students were already aware of it, which aligns with the teaching philosophy. But the teaching method should have been more of a demonstration or an experiential-based approach, as it engages most students and promotes hands-on learning. The availability of resources and teacher aids for experiments and demonstrations is to be answered, but science can't be learned orally. The second thing is more related to behaviour and how a teacher reacts to a particular situation and to students. In between, the students were changing their seats, exchanging water bottles, and making some noise. Later, I came across the fact that such disturbances are normal in the class, and students interrupt learning in other ways as well. There was a suggestion that I throw out students who were disrupting the class, but I restrained myself. Instead, I pretended to write the students' names to tell the coordinator, but in the end, I told the students I was acting. This might be helpful in the upcoming class; I think it helped me win the trust of a few annoying students. When I shared my experience of students making noise with a fellow prospective teacher, her response was "noise must have learning kaliya" (noise is necessary for learning). That is a perspective I need to understand, and I have to decide whether to give students the right to make noise and how much that should not affect learning. Lastly, this observation challenges the authority a teacher has over the students in the class until the end of the class. Today, during my lesson, a prefect and another boy disturbed my class three times. It is important to note that entering a class by anyone, even a coordinator, without the teacher's permission to give instructions and call out students breaks the rhythm, and it takes time to get back on track to teach again. Obviously, the prefect and the other boy were following the orders of other teachers and the management, who, I doubt, are not conscious of the disturbance they are making.</p>

<p>If I get a chance to teach the same topic again, I would choose the demonstration method over the inductive, as it at least helps the student to see the miracles of science instead of listening. After the demonstration, I would divide the students into different groups and lead a discussion to engage every single student.</p>
