Fourth row, third seat, a government primary school on the edge of the city. The boy has one pencil, worn down to a stub small enough to disappear inside his fist, and no sharpener. Beside him, a girl has nothing — no pencil, no pen, her notebook open to a blank page while the teacher writes a sentence on the board and asks the class to copy it down.

He doesn't announce anything. He simply writes three words, passes the stub sideways without looking at her, and keeps his eyes on the board, mouthing the next word to himself as if reading is easier when the lips move with it. She writes three words, passes it back. He writes three more. Back and forth, the whole sentence built in relay, neither of them missing a beat of the teacher's dictation, neither of them treating it as a negotiation.

No one taught them to do this today. It has the unhurried rhythm of something practiced — a system with its own unspoken rules, worked out long before I walked into the room with a clipboard and a set of questions about classroom resources that suddenly feel smaller than they did an hour ago.

The teacher finishes the sentence, turns, and starts the next one. The pencil keeps moving sideways, three words at a time, until the page is full — one pencil, one notebook each, and a sentence that took two children and no adult intervention at all to finish.
