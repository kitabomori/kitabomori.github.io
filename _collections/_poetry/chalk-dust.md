By four o'clock my sleeve has learned
the shape of every letter I wrote today —
a smudged equation on the left cuff,
a half a diagram, someone's name
I underlined for calling out an answer
no one else was brave enough to guess.

I used to wash it out at night,
scrub the white dust from the black cloth
like it was something to be ashamed of,
evidence I'd carried work back home.

Now I let it sit until the morning.
It reminds me, before the first bell rings,
that yesterday held thirty minds
mid-argument about a poem,
a boy who finally read a paragraph out loud
without his voice dropping to a whisper,
a question I could not answer
and told them so, and no one seemed to mind.

Chalk doesn't stay written long —
a board gets wiped, a lesson ends,
the room resets for the next hour
as if nothing happened here at all.

But the dust stays on the sleeve a little longer.
Small, grey, unglamorous proof
that something passed between us
that no attendance register will record.
