Walk into most secondary classrooms and you will find silent reading, worksheets, and the occasional group discussion — but rarely a teacher reading a text aloud to the class. Somewhere between primary school and secondary school, reading aloud quietly disappears from the timetable, treated as something children outgrow once they can decode words on their own. That assumption deserves a second look.

Reading aloud is not simply about decoding. When a teacher reads a complex passage aloud, they model fluency, pacing, and expression that a struggling reader cannot yet produce independently. They demonstrate how a skilled reader pauses at a comma, leans into a rhetorical question, or slows down for an unfamiliar word instead of skipping past it. For students who are still building vocabulary — which, in multilingual classrooms, can include a large share of any given class — hearing a text read well is often the difference between following an idea and losing it entirely.

There is also a social dimension that gets lost when reading becomes a purely private, silent act. A shared read-aloud gives a class a common reference point: a scene, a phrase, an argument that everyone in the room has now heard in the same voice, at the same pace. It creates space for a teacher to pause and ask a genuine question — not to test comprehension, but to think aloud together. That kind of shared attention is difficult to replicate when twenty-five students are each moving through a text silently and at different speeds.

None of this argues for reading aloud as a replacement for independent reading; older students clearly need extended, silent practice with texts to build stamina and comprehension at pace. The case here is narrower: that reading aloud has a place in secondary classrooms as a deliberate, occasional practice — for difficult texts, for poetry, for the opening chapter of a novel a teacher wants students to fall into rather than trudge through — and that dropping it entirely because it is associated with younger children costs us something real, particularly for the readers in the room who are least confident on their own.

The practical version of this is modest. A teacher does not need to read every text aloud, and does not need a special lesson block set aside for it. It can be five minutes at the start of a lesson with a passage that matters, followed by a genuine question rather than a comprehension check. It can be the first page of a new novel, read once by the teacher before students take it over. Small, occasional, and intentional — but present, rather than assumed away as something only younger children need.
