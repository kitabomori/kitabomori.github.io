Every few years, a new curriculum or a new set of textbooks is announced as the answer to Pakistan's reading outcomes, and every few years the underlying numbers barely move. Large-scale learning assessments have repeatedly found that a majority of children in the early grades of government schools cannot read a simple sentence in their own language, let alone comprehend a short paragraph. It is tempting to treat this as a curriculum problem — the wrong books, the wrong sequence of lessons — because a curriculum is something a ministry can rewrite in a single budget cycle. It is much harder to admit that the problem sits closer to the classroom floor than to the printing press.

Consider what a textbook reform actually changes. It changes the content a teacher is asked to deliver. It does not change whether that teacher had access to structured training in how to teach early reading, whether the class size makes individual attention possible, whether there is a functioning library within walking distance of the school, or whether books exist in the child's home language before they arrive at school in an unfamiliar one. A better textbook handed to an untrained teacher in an overcrowded, under-resourced classroom is still a better textbook sitting largely unused.

The evidence from years of donor-funded literacy pilots across Pakistan's provinces tells a consistent story: the interventions that move the needle are not glossy new books but unglamorous, sustained ones — structured phonics-based instruction, regular formative assessment that tells a teacher which specific child is struggling with which specific skill, and coaching support for teachers rather than one-off training workshops that are never followed up. None of this photographs well for a press release. All of it costs more, in recurring terms, than printing a new edition of a textbook.

There is also a library gap that rarely enters the policy conversation at all. A child who decodes words competently but never encounters a book worth finishing will not become a reader; decoding is necessary but not sufficient. Yet very few government schools in Pakistan have a functioning library, let alone a librarian or a rotating collection of age-appropriate storybooks. Reading, treated only as a technical skill to be tested rather than a habit to be built, produces students who can pass a reading assessment and still never choose to read.

None of this is an argument against updating textbooks, which do need periodic revision. It is an argument against treating a textbook reform as if it were, by itself, a literacy policy. A serious response to Pakistan's reading crisis would put comparable investment into three less visible places: sustained, coached teacher training in early-grade reading instruction; school libraries stocked with books children actually want to read, in languages they actually speak at home; and assessment systems that identify struggling readers early enough for a teacher to do something about it, rather than only measuring failure at the end of a school year.

Reading policy has an unfortunate tendency to reward whatever change can be announced quickly. Teachers, libraries, and early intervention are slower, less photogenic, and considerably more likely to work.
