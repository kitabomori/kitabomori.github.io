Practicum-I: A Teaching Reflection Journal

Thirteen weekly reflections and a final reflective report from Practicum-I at a government school, on classroom management, lesson planning, and what it means to become a teacher.

Click for audio on YouTube

This is a journal of thirteen weekly reflections written during Practicum-I at Sukkur IBA University, along with the final reflective report submitted at the end of the course. Each entry was written after a school visit to the Government (P) Municipal High School, Old Sukkur, and moves through a different theme — from the first nervous class to bullying, discipline, and what it actually takes to plan a lesson.

Reflection 1 — First Class

October 16, 2021

On the very first class of "Teaching Practice," each and every one of us in B.Ed 3rd semester was feeling uncomfortable, as usual before a new course. As the instructor entered the class, a few students rose in respect from their seats, and most remained still. The teacher was full of energy and greeted us with zeal and zest. Soon students settled down, and for a split second, silence filled the classroom. Next, the teacher arranged a chair for himself at the centre and began to introduce himself. Then it was time for the students to introduce themselves, and in between the instructor fired in some humorous questions, but the answers only provoked him to ask more. The very next moment, everyone was on their feet, laughing and screaming. As the introduction section ended, the teacher used a projector to introduce the course and its outline. At the same time, the course outline was demanding a lot of work ahead. Following that, the teacher highlighted the standards of teachers. Finally, at the end, the instructor taught us how to write a reflection and gave us the assignment of writing one on this very class.

Introducing himself in a humorous way eased the tense environment of the classroom. The energy of the teacher told the story of how passionate he is about his profession, which encouraged the students to consider adopting it too. The introduction and outline of the course helped us get familiar with what we were about to do in the coming days. Learning how to write a reflection added to our skills. The teacher's energy was at its peak even at the end of the class, which did not let students slip into gossip or lose attention. At the same time, the humorous style of asking questions made it a fun class — one I would love to be part of.

Reflection 2 — Writing Reflections & Setting Rules

October 13, 2021

The second practicum class of 3rd semester was held on the 13th of October in the smart classroom of the Knowledge Center. All the students were busy giving the final touches to their reflection of the previous class when the teacher arrived. He greeted us and gave utmost importance to one aspect of morality in his opening remarks: specific attention to the acknowledgement and appreciation of the efforts and contribution of an individual, particularly a teacher. Next, the teacher continued his lecture on writing a reflection, picking up where he left off in the last class. In the next section, he involved all the students in setting rules for the upcoming school visits. Initially, students had to come up with five rules each, with a word limit of five words. Next, we filtered out the rules by avoiding negative phrasing. Finally, we agreed on six rules with the instructor's help.

The work of educational institutions is not only to teach a few words, but the prime responsibility is to teach moral values, ultimately making a good citizen. The teacher's emphasis on praising someone's effort was the takeaway of the class — it is as simple as giving respect and taking respect. We have to appreciate individuals because it motivates them to do more, and better, next time. Secondly, I feel that making rules is as difficult as obeying them. It took almost half the class time for twenty-five students to agree on six rules, because we had to listen to everyone and then weigh the pros and cons. A class that was half teacher-centred and half student-centred benefited both: the teacher achieved his aim on time, and students enjoyed some freedom to participate in the learning process. Moving ahead, such an atmosphere would suit the objective of the course well.

Reflection 3 — First School Visit

October 20, 2021

For one final time before the school visit, we gathered in the smart classroom for the last set of instructions from Sir Sharik, who helped us understand how and what to observe at the school. It took almost 20 minutes to reach, and a while to find the Government (P) Municipal High School, Old Sukkur. The principal welcomed us and joined us for a group picture, and a staff member helped us navigate the school. Before the break, we visited and observed the library, science lab, computer lab, and finally the administration office and the surrounding area. During the break, I engaged with students, asking them about the school. Right after, we were paired up and sent to observe classrooms. I ended up inside 9th-A, where the teacher was teaching physics in Sindhi, and I took notes on the class. When the period ended, we left for the campus.

Overall, the school has the necessary resources to be run properly. The library has sufficient text and story books with enough space to accommodate a class, which could help students become self-learners. The science lab, with its apparatus from chemicals to microscopes and models to specimens, could help students learn by doing. The computer lab, meanwhile, could connect students to the vast knowledge of the world through the internet. In every institution, the administration works as the nucleus, monitoring the school's progress and managing resources for teachers and students. In the coming weeks of observation, I am keen to understand whether the administration, teachers, principal, and students are truly supporting each other in the learning process.

Reflection 4 — A Teacher's Movement in the Classroom

October 27, 2021

On the 27th of October, we were on the field for another observation, this time to note a teacher's movement inside the classroom and its impact on learning. When I entered class 9th-A, the teacher had already started the lesson. I found a place at the back and observed for about 20 minutes, noting his movement. After class, I asked him about the importance of a teacher's movement.

The seating arrangement and layout of a class are vital in deciding whether it becomes student-centred or teacher-centred. Class 9th-A was arranged in a grid of three columns and six rows. There was enough room between columns to move freely, but the rows were close together, leaving little room for engagement. During the lecture, the teacher stayed close to the board, but once he gave a question to solve, he moved freely around the class, often checking students' work by going to them. When I asked about this, he said, "It depends on the layout of the class and what kind of subject you are teaching." I agree, to an extent, but whatever the subject, it is important to be present in every part of the class so that no one feels left out. Observing this, I realised how important a teacher's movement is inside a classroom. To be a good teacher, one must develop this habit to thrive in the profession.

Reflection 5 — Classroom Management

November 3, 2021

This time, our task was to interview our cooperating teacher. After going through the observation sheet, my colleagues and I agreed on a few questions. When we asked for his time, he seemed to be trying to escape the interview, but after continuous insistence, he reluctantly agreed. The theme was classroom management.

The questions were fairly simple, but the responses were fragmented, and only a fraction of the information was related to what had been asked. When we asked, "What are your expectations from students for a lesson and by the end of the academic year?" his answer was simply "to perform better" — suggesting he was focused only on exam performance, not on skills or character building. On lesson planning, he said, "It sounds good to plan your lesson, but in reality it is of no use. I have enough experience to walk into class and start teaching." On behaviour and participation, he mostly blamed previous teachers for not building the basics, without offering any concrete strategies of his own.

From this interview, I understood that teaching in this school has stuck in the past — using methods from an earlier era to teach the mobile-phone generation of the 21st century. The profession demands that a teacher adopt current tactics and styles to manage students of the time. This experience is a reminder to be an updated teacher who never stops learning how to teach.

Reflection 6 — Observation to Create a Lesson Plan

November 10, 2021

"Observation to create a lesson plan" was the objective of today's visit. By the time we reached the classroom, the cooperating teacher had already started teaching a text from the English book about Shah Abdul Latif Bhitai, using the blackboard to write key points while reading aloud. He then asked students about Bhitai's background, name, early life, education, and spiritual growth. A couple of students answered by repeating what was written in the textbook, almost like parrots. The homework was to write a short paragraph about one aspect of Bhitai's life. Afterwards, we asked the teacher about his lesson plan, to see how far he had achieved his objective.

It was shocking to hear that he had not planned any lesson for several years. When asked how he then teaches a topic, he said, "I have enough experience to walk into a class and teach whatever the topic for the day is." Yet in the same breath, he emphasised the importance of lesson planning for moving a lesson forward systematically. Knowing the importance of something and still doing nothing is a formidable failing. A lesson plan helps focus on certain areas by allotting appropriate time to each segment, moving at a learner-directed pace toward the lesson's objective — preparing students at the start and evaluating what they've learned at the end.

Had I been in his place, I would have divided the text into chunks with clear objectives — for instance, that students would know about Bhitai's early life and education — and used material beyond the textbook, followed by group discussion where students share what they've learned, so the teacher can evaluate understanding. The homework could then connect this lesson to the next: reading Bhitai's poetry in the local language.

Reflection 7 — Classroom Management Observation: On/Off Task

November 24, 2021

This was the fifth visit to the school, with the purpose of recording when and why students pay attention in class. It was a mathematics class, as usual. Following the observation sheet, I selected ten students out of the thirty-one present and watched them across three instructions. During the first — writing a solved question from the board — nearly all ten stayed fully attentive. During the second — the teacher solving a question on the board — only four of the ten remained focused. During the third — the teacher assigning a question to solve — only half of the observed students engaged with it.

The outcome was that students stayed on task mainly during writing, while the rest of the activities didn't hold their attention effectively. When I shared this with the cooperating teacher, he said, "Mathematics is a subject that needs practice and practice, so this habit of note-taking helps them out." Still, it's the teacher's responsibility to engage every student through attention and clear communication. To maximise learning, a teacher needs a plan and the ability to judge what's lacking — formative assessment, for instance, could help check whether students are truly on task, alongside appropriate movement around the class to keep an eye on everyone.

Reflection 8 — Children Engagement Observation: Verbal Involvement

December 1, 2021

Today's goal was to see how much a teacher uses verbal involvement in class, and what effect it has on the lesson. The cooperating teacher arrived a bit late, which gave me time to review the observation sheet. When he arrived, he greeted everyone and asked a student for the mathematics textbook, then began a question on logarithm addition. While solving it, he asked several students what the next step would be, to keep them on task, and used both general and specific questions along with verbal instructions throughout.

Questioning, giving verbal instructions, and engaging students in healthy conversation are essential parts of teaching. Simply asking students how they are doing at the start of a lesson signals that a teacher cares — one of our most basic needs, per Maslow's hierarchy. Verbal involvement is vital for preparing a class, recalling the previous lecture, and connecting it to the next one. Clear verbal instruction motivates students to do better than the previous task and encourages active participation. On the other hand, when a teacher verbally engages only with a specific student or group, it can create a perception of bias or favouritism.

To be a good teacher, it is important to understand how vital it is to engage the whole class verbally. It helps manage disruptive behaviour, supports classroom management, and draws students into ongoing activities. Healthy, unbiased verbal engagement builds accountability, which is why it's highly recommended that teachers make an effort to involve every student in oral communication.

Reflection 9 — Interview of a Child

December 8, 2021

This was our seventh visit, this time to conduct a student interview. During the break, I asked Arshad Ali for his time, and with full zeal, he gave up part of his lunch break for my learning. I asked about his background, family and siblings, early education, the language he speaks, his favourite subject, his favourite teacher, hobbies, and friends.

Arshad came across as confident and content. He said this was the first time a teacher had taken interest in his life outside class. He was nervous at first but soon opened up, speaking confidently about his family. When it came to questions about the current school and teachers, though, he grew reluctant, and fear crossed his face — limiting his answers, which suggested students at this school aren't encouraged to speak freely about it. When talking about his friends, he seemed to be hiding something, which made me suspect some of his friendships weren't going well.

Interviewing a student is one way to truly know them. Once a teacher understands a student, they can build a genuine relationship — and plan lessons that match the student's intellect and capacity, in service of the lesson's objective.

Reflection 10 — Observation of a Child

December 15, 2021

Today's target was to observe a child, and I chose Arshad Ali again. From the moment I found him, he was engaged in some activity with the friend beside him — a healthy-looking child, of average height and build. Whenever the teacher gave instructions, Arshad tried to listen and write at the same time, which made me suspect he was only giving partial attention. At one point he borrowed a notebook from the student in front of him, and when the teacher assigned a question near the end of the maths class, he leaned on his friend's help. Thirty-five minutes weren't enough to draw conclusions about his strengths and talents, so I interviewed him during the break.

After talking with him, I got a clearer picture. He said he would prefer group study over the traditional lecture method — in group learning with peers, there's no language barrier or pressure to behave formally. He loves to sketch, though he feels no one really appreciates it, and he also loves cricket and other outdoor sports. He comes from a lower-middle-class background and has studied in government schools where teachers rarely focused on individual learning styles.

Teaching is not just about delivering a topic to a crowd; it's also about understanding the nature and behaviour of a child. That understanding helps a teacher recognise strengths, talents, and weaknesses, and shape a teaching style that suits how a student actually learns — planning lessons around their intellect to make them genuinely productive.

Reflection 11 — Discipline Procedures and Policies

December 15, 2021

This observation sheet's theme — discipline procedures and policies — led me to find out whether the school has any formal discipline policy. The administration's answer was "yes," but they couldn't produce any written document. That raised real questions: how does a school handle and prevent discipline issues without one, and who has the final authority over consequences? When I asked the cooperating teacher who provides guidance in the absence of written policy, he said, "The headmaster has the authority to act when such things happen, and, to some extent, a teacher has the power to bring things under control inside the classroom."

When a teacher holds ultimate authority over every decision, it can start to resemble a dictatorship, where students have no space to raise their voice. Some teachers lean on punishment to curb unwanted behaviour, others on warnings. Without a clear policy, decisions become inconsistent — shaped more by a teacher's mood than by the severity of the issue — which is exactly why a clear policy matters. The teacher also stressed the value of setting classroom norms at the start of the year and revisiting them periodically to prevent problems before they start. A discipline policy doesn't just prescribe consequences; it helps prevent issues from arising in the first place.

A teacher's role is to be a source of guidance for learners, and school policy should light that path. When a teacher's own values align with the school's policy, it builds confidence and a sense of ownership over the school and its students, which helps keep things from spiraling out of hand. In turn, students act within a shared circle of discipline, collaborating with teachers and peers — and that's how a genuine learning environment develops.

Reflection 12 — Observation of Lesson Plan (Performance Indicator)

January 12, 2024

Today's visit was about evaluating the cooperating teacher's performance against a lesson plan. I entered class 4th-A, where Sir Abdul Majeed was teaching mathematics as usual. The observation sheet covered several performance areas: lesson planning, subject competency, communication skills, classroom and time management, learning environment, interpersonal skills, and assessment.

Mr Abdul Majeed scored 68 out of 100 on the rating scale — solidly in the "good teacher" range. He performed well in several areas but fell short on lesson planning, classroom management, and assessment. He clearly had a strong grip on content knowledge, with over twenty years of experience, and with updated lesson planning and more innovative teaching methods, he could meaningfully improve student participation.

To be an effective teacher requires strength across several key areas — from planning a lesson to delivering it with clear instruction and communication, alongside fair evaluation of students. Expertise in content knowledge alone, without attention to method, isn't enough. Teaching in this century demands both: knowing the subject well and understanding the students in front of you.

Reflection 13 — Bullying in the School Environment

January 12, 2024

On January 12, we made our last school visit, this time to understand bullying — its causes, its several forms, and how it shows up in the context of this school. The observation sheet helped structure what to look for on the school premises.

Bullying is often defined as a repeated, habitual use of verbal or physical strength by peers or seniors to harm others emotionally or physically. It has serious effects on both the bully and the bullied: over time, the bully can become an oppressor who violates norms and laws, while the bullied often faces isolation, depression, low self-esteem, and, in extreme cases, thoughts of self-harm. Bullying can be driven by race, religion, social class, appearance, physical or mental strength, size, age, or gender, and it takes verbal, physical, sexual, and cyber forms. Some bullies act alone, others in groups, and some seem to draw real satisfaction from causing others suffering or humiliation — a kind of sadism that finds pleasure in mocking others. At times, victims of bullying go on to become bullies themselves, out of a need for revenge. Most bullying at school happens in classrooms, during breaks, and in play areas, though it's increasingly showing up on social media too. The tragedy of bullying is that it happens all around us, directly or indirectly, and countering it takes real, sustained effort from everyone involved, from the individual level up.

Within a school, it is a teacher's prime responsibility to notice what's going on with a particular student. A student's withdrawn or troubled expression can be a sign that someone is being bullied or victimised. A teacher should talk to that student personally, offer reassurance that they can be a source of comfort, and work to resolve the problem before it escalates.

Practicum-I Reflective Report

The third semester of B.Ed-III, at Sukkur IBA University, started in October 2021. Alongside other courses, Practicum-I was part of the semester — a course unique in giving prospective teachers direct experience of the school environment through weekly visits. Its objectives included connecting teaching theory with teaching practice, collaborating with peers, cooperating teachers, school staff, and the practicum supervisor, and observing every element of a school, from the gatekeeper to the principal. A large part of it was also learning to invite, accept, and act on feedback from the cooperating teacher, supervisor, and peers.

The practicum ran across 16 weeks. The first two were spent understanding what the practicum involved, what we would be doing in the coming weeks, and how to write a good reflection. The following ten weeks took us to the Government (P) Municipal High School, Old Sukkur, each visit built around a different observation objective, with a reflection written after each one to track learning and progress. The remaining three weeks were dedicated to reflecting on and presenting our learning across three seminars, before finally writing this reflective report to sum up the semester.

A school is not just a building; it's a place where students get an education, and a good education depends on a good environment — both in people (teachers, management) and infrastructure (classrooms, labs, a library, washrooms, play areas, and basic facilities down to clean drinking water and first aid). Observing this school from different angles taught me how its various sections interlink, and how essential that chain is to the school functioning well. The visits and the process of reflecting drew my focus to several recurring themes:

  1. The role of management in running a school smoothly.
  2. The use of available resources for student learning.
  3. How essential the movement of a teacher is inside the classroom.
  4. What encourages a student to participate in class.
  5. The importance of lesson planning.
  6. Tactics to check whether students are attentive — questioning, asking for opinions and suggestions, on-the-spot reflections, and so on.
  7. The value of giving clear verbal instructions.
  8. Ensuring every student is genuinely involved in learning.
  9. Why it matters to understand the psyche of students.
  10. The importance of policy documents so a school can handle misconduct impartially.
  11. What it takes to evaluate a teacher's performance, and the range of expertise a good teacher needs.
  12. A teacher's responsibility to know what's happening in a student's life — whether they're doing well, or being bullied.

In previous semesters, I had studied the theories of education: how to manage a classroom, which methods suit which context, and whether a student- or teacher-centred classroom is more suited to the 21st century. Practicum-I let me test and verify those theories in the field, and comparing theory against reality taught me more than either could alone.

Across the semester, I met nearly everyone at the school in some capacity. Conversing with them, and reflecting on the practicum's objectives against the observation sheets, helped me actually achieve them — and achieving a goal builds the confidence to push past the next boundary. The first five weeks were shaky and unclear, and I genuinely felt the practicum might be a pointless exercise. By the second five weeks, the rationale behind the course became clear: this was about developing certain skills and qualities by observing cooperating teachers and the school itself. From there on, I felt genuinely satisfied for the rest of the semester, enjoyed sharing what I'd learned with classmates, and learned just as much from them in return.

Looking back, the practicum was a real success for me — I largely met the targets I'd set. The seminars pushed me to think more deeply about what I'd actually been doing throughout the process. In hindsight, especially in that early phase, I felt like I was going nowhere and that the course was little more than a formality. As time passed, the objectives became clear, and presenting my experience to the class confirmed that I had observed what was needed, and learned from it.

The semester gave me a real opportunity to build certain skills, while also surfacing a few strengths and shortcomings in how I work. I learned that I can stay focused on the task in front of me and handle pressure well, and that when responsibility is handed to me, I can offer my best by being cooperative and staying impartial when it's needed. Some areas of concern in how I work also came up through my colleagues' feedback — it was strange, at first, to sit with the idea that I don't always give enough weight to other people's opinions. On the other hand, I don't tend to celebrate small wins individually, but I do notice how a string of small moments and minor successes eventually adds up to something bigger.

Skills I developed during this practicum include:

  1. A more polite, professional manner of communicating with colleagues, peers, and people generally.
  2. A stronger instinct to question things that aren't moving in the right direction.
  3. Writing a noticeably better reflection than when I started.
  4. Presenting data and learnings more concisely during presentations.
  5. A better sense of the value of doing things on time.
  6. Organising, saving, and storing data for later use on platforms like Google Drive.
  7. Modest improvement in my spoken and written English.

There is always room to grow, and as a prospective teacher, I believe in aiming to be well-rounded. Areas I still want to develop include:

  1. Improving oral communication, alongside continued work on writing.
  2. Building stronger presentations using the resources available.
  3. Developing my ability to work in a team, and my leadership skills.

There is little use in becoming a teacher who isn't intrinsically motivated. If anything felt missing across the practicum, it was the kind of motivation and passion that comes from seeing a cooperating teacher genuinely devoted to their profession. The school we visited was, in many ways, a place where anyone arriving with high hopes could end up disheartened. Next time I get the chance to visit a school, I want to ask cooperating teachers directly why they teach. Beyond that, the tactics and techniques covered by the course were solid — what I came away with, most of all, is a clearer sense of the dimensions of a school.
