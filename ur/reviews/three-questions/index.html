Three Questions

Lev Nikolayevich Tolstoy (1828-1910) believed that novels serve as tools for psychological education and reform, helping us understand one another, especially those who may seem unappealing at first glance. As the master of realistic fiction, he wrote acclaimed works such as “War and Peace,” “Anna Karenina,” and the novella “The Death of Ivan Ilyich,” which is considered one of his finest. His idea of nonviolent resistance, as expressed in “The Kingdom of God Is Within You,” has profoundly influenced 20th-century figures, including Mahatma Gandhi and Martin Luther King Jr. In “The Three Questions,” part of the compilation “What Men Live By and Other Tales,” Tolstoy offers deep, insightful perspectives on various aspects of human life.

One day, a king realised that the answers to three simple questions could lead to success in every task he undertook. To encourage participation, he announced a reward for the correct answers. People from all walks of life offered various responses: for “What is the right time to begin anything?” answers ranged from calendars and schedules to magicians. Similarly, responses to “Who are the right people to listen to and whom should we avoid?” varied widely. Some suggested listening to counsellors, doctors, and experts in their respective fields. Lastly, for “What are the most important things to do?”, no satisfactory answers were found.

When no one received the reward, the king sought the wisdom of a hermit who lived simply in the wilderness. Upon encountering the hermit, the king repeated his questions, but the hermit was busy digging. By the afternoon, the king still had not received any answers. Just then, an injured man came running toward them. The king promptly tended to the man’s injuries. The following morning, the injured man apologised and revealed that he had intended to seek revenge against the king, which had led to his injury at the hands of his guards. The king forgave him and restored his property.

As the king prepared to leave without answers, the hermit said, “You have already been answered. When I was digging yesterday, you helped me, and that was the most important time. I was the most important man, and doing good for me was the most important business. Then, when the injured man came, attending to him became the most important task, or else he could have died. He was the most important man at that moment, and what you did for him was the most important action.”

Tolstoy firmly believed that literature and the arts could be used for psychological education and reform. The story of the three questions reflects his beliefs and thoughts. If we focus on living in the present, value those around us, and prioritise our actions, we can overcome many obstacles. Tolstoy summarises his argument: “Remember, there is only one time that is important—’ now.’ It is the most crucial time because it is the only time when we have any power. The most necessary person is the one you are with, as no one knows if they will ever have to engage with another. And the most important affair is to do good for that person because man was sent into this life for that purpose.” A century later, Paulo Coelho echoed these sentiments in “The Alchemist,” stating, “I don’t live in either my past or my future. I’m interested only in the present. If you can always concentrate on the present, you’ll be a happy man. Life will be a party for you, a grand festival.”

By applying the philosophy of the three questions, we can lead lives free of hassle and depression. For instance, consider students in a class. If they occupy their minds with distractions outside class, such as social media and gossip, they learn little and end up blaming external factors for their struggles. Their priority should be the teacher, their classmates, and the topic at hand.
